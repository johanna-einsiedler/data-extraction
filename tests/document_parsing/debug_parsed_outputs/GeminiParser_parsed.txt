Related Work\n\nThe field of biomedical image analysis has seen significant advancements, driven by the increasing availability of high-resolution imaging data and the development of sophisticated computational techniques. This section provides an overview of existing methods, categorizing them into traditional, deep learning, and hybrid approaches, highlighting their strengths and limitations.\n\n### 2.1 Traditional Methods\n\nTraditional methods for biomedical image analysis often rely on handcrafted features and classical machine learning algorithms. These approaches typically involve several steps: pre-processing, feature extraction, and classification or segmentation. For instance, edge detection algorithms like Canny (Canny, 1986) and Sobel (Sobel, 1970) are widely used for identifying boundaries in images. Morphological operations, such as erosion and dilation, are employed for noise reduction and object shape manipulation.\n\nCommonly used traditional techniques include:\n*   **Thresholding**: A simple yet effective method for segmenting objects from the background based on pixel intensity values (Otsu, 1979). Adaptive thresholding methods adjust the threshold dynamically across the image.\n*   **Region Growing**: Starts with a seed point and iteratively adds neighboring pixels that satisfy a predefined similarity criterion, such as intensity or color similarity (Adams & Bischof, 1994).\n*   **Clustering**: Unsupervised methods like K-means (MacQueen, 1967) or Expectation-Maximization (EM) (Dempster et al., 1977) are used to group pixels into distinct clusters, which often correspond to different tissue types or structures.\n*   **Active Contours (Snakes)**: Deformable models that minimize an energy function to delineate object boundaries. They are effective for smooth contours but can be sensitive to initialization and noise (Kass et al., 1988).\n\n### 2.2 Deep Learning Approaches\n\nThe advent of deep learning has revolutionized biomedical image analysis, offering unparalleled performance in various tasks. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (LeCun et al., 1998) are particularly effective due to their ability to automatically learn hierarchical features from raw image data. For classification tasks, architectures like AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014), and ResNet (He et al., 2016) have achieved state-of-the-art results. These models typically consist of multiple convolutional layers followed by pooling layers and fully connected layers, outputting a probability distribution over classes. Given an input image $I \\in \\mathbb{R}^n$, a CNN learns a mapping $f: \\mathbb{R}^n \\to \\mathbb{R}^k$ where $k$ is the number of classes.\n\nFor segmentation, U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) and its variants have become the de facto standard. U-Net\'s symmetric encoder-decoder architecture with skip connections allows for precise localization and segmentation of structures, even with limited training data. These models combine the strengths of both paradigms.
